Shackleton Rover

The heroic age of polar exploration ended in 1922, but the achievements remain with us today: an enduring British presence in Antarctica that has driven global benefits for a hundred years.

These efforts have helped to ensure that the continent remains a global commons, and underpin the conservation of the great whales, the protection of the ozone layer, and the defence of freedom in the South Atlantic. The value has been incalculable.

A new age of exploration is beginning. The UK, echoing this success, should ensure that the Moon becomes a new global commons with environmental protections, free navigation, and a fair balance between initiative, risk, and return. This recognises the moral responsibilities that emerge alongside the scientific, strategic, and economic outcomes which inevitably cluster towards the first movers.

We have an opportunity to help create this positive scenario. However, as in Antarctica, we must demonstrate our intent through a physical presence on the ground. We therefore propose the Shackleton lunar rover as an ambitious goal.
